Meeting Notes

1. Principles of Good Design

Dieter Rams – Ten Principles

  1. Innovative – Not dependent on technological advances; design is adaptable and remains relevant over time.
  1. Useful – Meets functional, psychological, and aesthetic criteria.
  1. Aesthetic – Well executed and visually appealing.
  1. Understandable – Self-explanatory and easy to interpret.
  1. Unobtrusive – Neutral and restrained, allowing space for user self-expression.
  1. Honest – Does not attempt to deceive or manipulate.
  1. Long-lasting – Avoids trends and short-lived fashions.
  1. Thorough – Accurate, showing respect for the user.
  1. Environmentally friendly – Conserves resources; minimises physical and visual pollution.
  1. Minimal – “Less but better”; focuses on essential aspects, aiming for purity and simplicity.

3. Design Philosophy

  • Good cartographic design is about effective communication, not rigid rules or tradition.
  • Aim to inform and, where possible, delight the user (Rosenfeld, 2010).
  • Consider:
    • Map scale and resolution
    • Colour mode (RGB for screen, CMYK for print)
    • Paper/screen size and type
    • Interactivity and functionality
  • Use desaturated base map colours so overlaid data stands out.
  • Ensure location names are clear for quick recognition during searches.
  • Select line weights, fonts, and symbol sizes that remain legible at the given scale.
  • Consider overlapping products or datasets and how they interact visually.

4. Data Quality, Accessibility, and Compliance

  • Existence metadata – Whether datasets and services have associated metadata.
  • Conformity metadata – Whether metadata and spatial data meet implementing rules.
  • Coverage – Percentage of relevant territory covered by datasets.
  • Accessibility – Ability to view and download datasets.
  • WCAG compliance – Colour contrast, keyboard navigation, alt text (audited via Lighthouse, axe, DevTools).
  • Legal certainty – Compliance with laws and cultural norms.
  • Independence – Extent of independent data sources and author control.
  • Corruption prevention – Protection from private interest abuse.
  • Public participation – Mechanisms for user involvement.
  • Social inclusion – Trustworthiness and openness.
  • Societal mediation – Ability for users to communicate.
  • Conflict management – Support, cross-checking, feedback channels.
  • Education – Provision of new or overlooked information.
  • Research and innovation – Support for further development and new products/services.
  • Employment – Integration with existing workflows; ease of training.
  • Environmental sustainability – Inclusion of environmental considerations.

5. Usability and Performance Testing

Performance Metrics

  • Load time: <4s on broadband, <8s on 4G mobile.
  • Interaction lag: ≤0.5s when toggling layers or moving the map.
  • Export time: <10s for medium datasets.
  • Task simulation: Locate a dataset layer within 20 seconds.
  • Reduction in time to find/export datasets compared to existing portals.

Functionality Checks

  • Side-by-side comparison of exported vs in-platform maps.
  • Test layer clarity against OS, Bertin, and Tufte guidelines.
  • Evaluate user success rates for planners vs public.
  • Check mobile responsiveness (desktop, tablet, smartphone).
  • Map layer switching: road map, satellite, other views.
  • Prioritisation for urban designers.
  • Policy coordination: Combining layers to support planning policies.

Data Verification

  • Compare intended vs integrated datasets.
  • Check timestamps against original sources.
  • Identify formats, licences, and APIs for data integration.
  • Document inconsistencies in naming, CRS, and metadata.

6. Feature Exploration

  • Prototype in-platform commenting tied to coordinates/polygons.
  • Investigate annotation workflows in planning (planners, architects, public).
  • Symbology tooltips, legends, and simplified views to improve map literacy.
  • Compare government map styling to Bertin’s semiology and Tufte’s clarity principles for standardisation.
  • Explore multiple authority data publishing: local councils, Historic England, Environment Agency, OS OpenData.
  • Urban analytics data infrastructure: critical spatial data infrastructure for urban management.
  • Measure and compare data transfer workflows (QGIS, ArcGIS, AutoCAD, web viewers).

7. Broader Questions

  • Why are new technologies essential for urban design and planning, locally (e.g., Canterbury) and more generally?
  • What are the positive and negative impacts?
  • What issues could arise in future implementation?
  • How innovative and flexible is the platform?
  • Is it easy to implement and master?
  • Is it timely, clear, and free from confusion?
