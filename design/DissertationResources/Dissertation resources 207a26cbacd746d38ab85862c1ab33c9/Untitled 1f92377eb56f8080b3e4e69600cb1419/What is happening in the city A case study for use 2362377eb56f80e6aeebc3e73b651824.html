What is happening in the city? A case study for user-centred geovisualisation design

Links		 https://doi.org/10.1080/17489725.2019.1630680

Users of location-based social networks have been producing geographicaldata continually for the last years. This data have been used by researchers invarying contexts (Zheng, Xie, and Ma. 2010; Scellato et al. 2011) includingunderstanding mobility patterns (Cheng et al. 2011), crowd detection (Lee andSumiya 2010), and friend-based location recommendation applications Ye, Yin,and Lee (2010); Fujisaka, Lee, and Sumiya (2010) used geotagged tweets tounderstand urban characteristics. Similarly, Ferrari et al. (2011) extracted urbanhotspots in the city using 13 million Tweets. Using cell phone data, Calabreseet al. (2010) described the relationship between user’s home location andsocial events that they go.Ciuccarelli, Lupi, and Simeone (2014) combined diﬀerent social mediasources in a uniﬁed visualisation to understand urban semantic layers andtheir change over time. Geographical data can be used to create meaning fora wide variety of purposes. In our study, we will investigate how citizens maycontribute to the meaning-creation for their everyday purposes of the city

The primary focus of this study is on the geovisualisation and our mainapproach while designing the system, is to create a simple and easy userinterface (UI). Similar to commonly used web map applications like GoogleMaps, the primary focus of the web page is on the map area, while the usercan ﬁlter, search or navigate using controls on the left sidebar. UI of the mapconsists of geovisualisation area (multilayered visualisations of events, crowddata, traﬃc data and weather data), Sidebar includes a search bar, time ﬁlter,and switches for the diﬀerent data layers (events, crowd, traﬃc, and weather).When a user opens the site, only the event layer is visible by default. Sliderscontrol the ﬁlters under events, except for language ﬁlter which can be ﬁlteredby checkboxes. The temporal ﬁlter has text input ﬁelds with a calendar toselect a range. Additionally, there are three buttons to choose quickly commontime ranges (today, tomorrow, this weekend). To ﬁlter data spatially, users canpan and zoom, or use the search bar to ﬁnd places by name.

We hereby explore the design space of visualising overlaid, multi-layered databy collecting user insight about two combinations of geovisualisation techni-ques. Both variations are informationally equivalent, and both visually overlaysfour data layers on the same base map. This technique (Translucent Overlay) isan eﬃcient inter-layer visual comparison technique (Lobo, Pietriga, and Appert2015). The base map in both pages uses a minimalist style with water in paleblue and land in white.Superimposed data layers are events, crowd, traﬃc, and weather. Thesedata layers can be made visible or invisible through the left sidebar. On theﬁrst layer, information about planned events in the city is available. One of theusers needs from previous studies was being able to see live data of peopledistribution in the area, along with event information. In the people layer, livegeo-tagged tweets from Twitter are visualised to represent people distributionin the city. The traﬃc layer shows real-time information on traﬃc density andspeed, collected from Google Maps APIs (2017). On the weather layer, informa-tion which might aﬀect the decision for going to an event is visualised like rainor snow, as requested by users. Many of today’s web-based maps, spatial dataare represented with markers. For the ﬁrst prototype called Pin (see Figure 1),we used markers to represent events. We used colour coding to representa diﬀerent genre of events. Even though markers are simple and commonlyused, they only mark the location and do not provide any further detailedinformation at ﬁrst sight. Additionally, when markers are overlapped, an issueof visibility arises as identiﬁed by Meier and Heidmann (2014).For the second prototype called Circle (See Figure 2), we used a custom circularglyph design to represent events (See Figure 3). As the user zooms in, the circlesget more detailed to represent more dimensions of data. The most detailedversion of the circle consists of six visual variables. Fuchs et al. (2017) state thatincreasing the level of dimensions negatively aﬀects the performance of dataglyphs. Authors also point out that as the number of data points decreases, searchwithin a data set is positively impacted. Because of this, the data dimensions of theglyph we designed for Circle increase as the user zooms into an area.We used the same visualisation methods for Pin and Circle for the threesupportive data layers which are traﬃc, people density, and weather. Forrepresenting people distribution in the area, we used dot density map as itis a well-established method for visualising people density (Arthur Robinson1958; Slocum et al. 2008; Zuniga, Pueyo, and Calvo. 2012). For the traﬃc layer,we used a monochromatic-red scale for road lines. We collectedtraﬃc data from Google Maps API, and weather data from DarkSky API(DarkSky 2017). There are still other types of data (sound of the event, physicalactivity level of the event) that we included in the interface as they were partsof user needs, but we did not collect any data related to them yet.

We recruited 16 volunteer participants (eight females and eight males)through word-of-mouth from our University. Participants were not connectedwith previous studies, and all of them were citizens of Istanbul. Participantswere aged between 20 and 37 (Median: 23). None of them had colour-blindness. As we wanted our participants to reﬂect the heterogeneity ofcitizens, their backgrounds ranged widely. We had nine university studentsfrom ranging ﬁelds including media and visual arts, business administration,international relations, psychology and computer science. We also had a PhDstudent, three architects, a customer director, a designer and a movie director.Except for three participants, none of the participants were trained in the ﬁeldsof geography, cartography, geographical information systems, graphic design,or ﬁne arts. All participants stated that they were experienced in using webmaps. Most of the participants used web maps daily or weekly. All of theparticipants noted they use maps to ﬁnd an address, 14 participants use webmaps to plan a route, 5 of them uses to measure distances or areas, and 3 ofthem uses web maps for fun. Thirteen of the participants stated that they goout for an event daily or weekly while 3 participants do this less often.Participants noted that they ﬁnd out about events mostly through socialmedia or friends. The sessions were conducted on a 13-in. MacBook 2015,using a mouse. We used Quicktime 7 software for screen recording and soundrecording.
