Multidimensional Web GIS Approach for Citizen
Participation on Urban Evolution

Links		https://doi.org/10.3390/ijgi8060253

The aim of our research was to design and develop a multidimensional web-mapping platform—supporting 2D, 3D, and 4D visualizations—to help users better understand the temporal evolution of their cities. Built using Cesium, an open-source virtual globe framework powered by JavaScript and WebGL, the platform combines a web-based questionnaire with dynamic spatial visualizations to enhance citizen engagement and participation. The resulting prototype serves as a proof of concept and offers a customizable framework suitable for various projects with minimal development time. In doing so, our work responds to the call by Resch et al. [13,20] for more studies exploring intuitive 4D representations of spatiotemporal phenomena on the web. We also propose a simulation mechanism that supports 2D and 3D web visualizations while fulfilling the requirements of participatory planning. This research builds upon previous work [21] by incorporating spatiotemporal simulations in citizen-driven consultations to better visualize change and its impacts.

Web-mapping has significantly evolved over the past decade—from simple 2D representations to more immersive 3D environments—largely due to advances in WebGL. Alatalo et al. [22] identify WebGL as a valuable technology for developing visualizations in participatory urban planning. Virtual environments such as Unity, X3DOM, and Three.js have been commonly used to build public-facing tools [13,15,20,22], while virtual globes like NASA World Wind, Cesium.js, and Skyline have proven effective in participatory contexts [19,23–29]. These tools replicate familiar features from platforms like Google Earth, such as zooming, panoramic views, and project visualizations, which enhance accessibility for the general public. Lovett et al. [30] emphasize the potential of 3D visualizations in overcoming communication and mediation challenges in planning, though they stress the need for further studies to fully assess their effectiveness and to evaluate the added value of emerging technologies.

System performance is a critical factor in engaging the public—slow or unresponsive tools can deter participation. While WebGL has become the standard for 3D graphics on the web, it still lags in performance compared to native Windows applications, with a slowdown factor of two to five times [22]. Rendering large 3D datasets remains a technical challenge [15,22]. Chaturvedi et al. [31] proposed a tile-based CityGML rendering strategy via 3DCityDB, enabling efficient visualization in Cesium.js and Google Earth. Although Cesium’s 3D Tiles have emerged as a promising solution for online rendering of large city models, they still lacked support for direct CityGML conversion during the time of our research.

The ability to represent space at multiple levels of abstraction offers clear benefits throughout the planning and decision-making process [32]. According to Dambruch et al. [15], 3D models should be highly realistic to be comprehensible and persuasive in consultation settings. Conversely, Alatalo et al. and Semmo et al. [22,33] argue that abstract models can enhance performance and improve information recognition depending on the context. Both 2D and 3D representations have advantages: while 3D provides greater realism and intuitive understanding [17,19], Tory et al. [18] suggest that the combination of both is most effective for problem-solving.

To create a time-dynamic participatory platform, two essential components must be addressed: methods for spatiotemporal analysis and effective time representation. Zheng et al. [34] outline key spatiotemporal visual analytics for urban computing, with data filtering being one of the most effective techniques [35]. Filtering enhances performance and reduces visual clutter by limiting the number of displayed layers. Time is typically represented linearly along a timeline [36], supported by animations that reveal patterns in temporal data [37]. Changes are conveyed through visual variables such as color, height, texture, or through the appearance and disappearance of layers [24,38]. For instance, Yin and Shiode [17] used separate 3D models for different time steps and called for more research exploring how changes in 3D models correlate with variables like population growth. Echoing Resch et al. [20], we stress the need for more evaluations of 4D web-based visualizations to ensure they are understandable to the public.

A user-friendly interface is essential for successful citizen participation. Resch et al. [20] provide initial usability criteria for 3D/4D web maps, aligned with ISO9241-11 standards that define usability metrics and applications [39]. Interfaces modeled on familiar tools such as Google Maps and Earth offer intuitive navigation and enhance public accessibility. Our platform incorporates a web-based questionnaire as the primary engagement tool, offering a fast and cost-effective means for institutions to solicit citizen feedback. Compared to open-text responses, questionnaires also enable quicker statistical analysis [15].

To avoid cognitive overload, we excluded space–time cube visualizations (which represent time along a Z-axis), as they are often too complex for the general public to interpret [42]. Instead, we opted for more comprehensible methods, including linear timelines, animations, charts, and data filtering. Timelines provide a straightforward representation of time [36], while animations clearly communicate temporal changes but are best used sparingly to avoid overloading users [37]. We used color coding to depict different time steps and adopted grayscale environments to enhance perceptual contrast [37]. Charts, while traditionally used for statistics, also served as spatiotemporal analysis tools by explicitly linking animations and providing additional insights. Filtering allowed users to isolate specific time periods or data subsets, reducing visual complexity and enhancing clarity [24,35].

The platform interface consists of five main components: a questionnaire, a header, a toolbar, the main visualization scene, and a clock with an accompanying timeline. While Cesium API provides many built-in features, we developed additional functionalities such as switching between abstract (dark, simplified) and photorealistic (textured) building visualizations using Mapbox and Bing imagery. Users could toggle visual layers like 3D buildings and charts. Interaction tools included standard scene navigation (pan, zoom, rotate), as well as hover, click, and multi-click functions. Hovering highlighted objects; clicking displayed attribute data; and multi-click allowed users to select multiple features—useful in real-time consultations.

Participants could switch between abstraction levels, and this feature was generally well received. It not only provided contextual detail but also improved performance by reducing graphical demand. However, we found that this feature should be made more prominent and potentially customizable, as suggested by Semmo et al. [33]. Developers should assess its suitability for each consultation scenario to avoid unnecessary development efforts.

Although the approach needs further testing with a broader and more representative sample, our findings suggest that the platform could be highly beneficial for fostering citizen participation. In practice, 2D polyline representations may be sufficient for infrastructure elements like roads, bike lanes, and public transit. Conversely, buildings are better evaluated using 3D models, which are more intuitive for users. Furthermore, abstract concepts like population density are most effectively communicated through 3D spatiotemporal visualizations. These insights underscore the potential of multidimensional web-mapping tools to enhance participatory urban planning and decision-making.
