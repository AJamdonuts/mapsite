The surge in the amount, variety, and complexity has led
to remotely sensed data being recognized as “Geo-Big Data.”
This designation refects its defning traits: volume, with
an ever-growing reservoir of archived and acquired data;
velocity, driven by the rapid pace of data generation and the
demand for real-time processing; variety, as data streams
from a multitude of satellite sensors, each with unique spectral, spatial, temporal, and radiometric resolutions; veracity,
highlighting challenges in data accuracy and uncertainty;
and value, signifying the wealth of insights waiting to be
extracted (Sedona et al. 2019). The escalating volume and
complexity of geo-big data present formidable challenges
to current systems from an operational standpoint. Cloud
computing platforms, powered by supercomputers, provide
robust and efcient solutions for geospatial big data storage, processing, and analysis (Pham-Duc et al. 2023). The
computing platforms function as virtualized supercomputing
infrastructures, replicate the characteristics of physical computers but ofer enhanced fexibility in processing power,
memory allocation, and disk storage confgurations (Waleed
and Sajjad 2023; Tamiminia et al. 2020; Ma et al. 2014). The
most popular cloud computing services for processing large
amounts of geospatial data are Microsoft Azure, Amazon
Web Services (AWS), Google Cloud Platform (GCP), and
Google Earth Engine (GEE) (Tamiminia et al. 2020; PhamDuc et al. 2023).
