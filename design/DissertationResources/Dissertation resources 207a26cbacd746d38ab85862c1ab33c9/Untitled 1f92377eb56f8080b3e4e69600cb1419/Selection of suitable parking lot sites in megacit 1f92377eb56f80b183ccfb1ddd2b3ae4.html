Selection of suitable parking lot sites in megacities: A case study for four districts of Istanbul

Links		https://doi.org/10.1016/j.landusepol.2021.105731
  • In most cases, it is hard to think about SDSS separately from GIS because GIS offers various tools for spatial data handling (Sugumaran and DeGroote, 2011). In this context, MCDMtechniques are often incorporated into GIS. This combination is commonly known as GIS-MCDM. With GIS-MCDM approach, it is possible to integrate multiple criterion layers of candidate locations or sites, taking into account the priorities of decision makers for a spatial decision problem. The resulting GIS layer (output map) shows the locations or sites ranked by their suitability (Malczewski and Rinner, 2015).
  • One of the most used method for MCDM is analytical hierarchy process (AHP) but this method cannot address the imprecise judgements of decision makers. In this respect, fuzzy AHP, which integrates the concepts of fuzzy set theory and hierarchical structure analysis, can be used to reduce or even eliminate the fuzziness and vagueness existing in many decision-making problems (Emrouznejad and Ho, 2017, Percin, 2008; Taskin Gumus et al., 2013; Yayla et al., 2015). Fuzzy AHP has been playing an increasingly important role in MCDM under uncertainty and has found extensive applications in a wide variety of areas (Emrouznejad and Ho, 2017, Wang and Chin, 2011). Due to its advantages, fuzzy AHP is preferred as the MCDM method in this study.
  • Spatial data used in the study were compiled from IMM City Information System and ISPARK database (Table 2). Spatial data are held in feature classes in GIS. Each feature class has a vector geometry (point, line or polygon) or raster geometry (grid) and one or more attributes relevant to parking lot site selection.
  • Determination of the resolution (grid cell size) is also important in terms of spatial precision of the analysis. In this regard, the recommendation of Hengl (2006) as well as the level of detail of compiled spatial data were taken into account. According to Hengl (2006), the ideal resolution is 2.5 m (finest: 0.5 m, coarsest: 12.5 m) since most of the spatial data are large-scaled (around 1:5 000). On the other hand, the slope data had a relatively lower resolution as well as some of the attribute data were just available at neighborhood level. Therefore, the resolution of the criterion layers was decided to be 5 m in this study.
