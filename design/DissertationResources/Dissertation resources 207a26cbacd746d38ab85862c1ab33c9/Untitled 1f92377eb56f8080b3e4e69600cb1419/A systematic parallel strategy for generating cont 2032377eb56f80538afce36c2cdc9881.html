A systematic parallel strategy for generating
contours from large-scale DEM data using
collaborative CPUs and GPUs

Links		https://doi.org/10.1080/15230406.2020.1854862
  • Contour maps play an important role in the analysis and modeling of terrains in the field of geographic informa- tion systems (GISs) (Jones, 2014). In recent years, the use of raster digital elevation models (DEMs) has increased (Jiang et al., 2013). Compared with raster DEM data, the contours in vector format are more measurable; conse- quently, generating contour lines from a DEM grid is becoming quite practical (Jones et al., 2000). However, this process is computationally intensive, and the com- monly used algorithms include the popular marching squares, Theo Pavlidis, and linear interpolation algo- rithms (Carsten, 2003; Chester et al., 1968; Pavlidis, 2012). Some researchers have investigated strategies to accelerate the process based on these algorithms through fast contour sorting (Jones et al., 2000; Nickerson et al., 1999; Yates, 1987) or contour traces (Wang, 2010; Wang et al., 2007; Zhao et al., 2014). Other researchers have also improved the accuracy of the process by using different interpolation functions, such as the polynomials and Lagrange functions (Schoenberg, 1969; Schoenberg & Sharma, 1973). In addition, several research efforts have focused on the development of effective compressed encoding for contour lines (Miyatake et al., 1997), con- version from different file formats of DEM data (Hiremath & Kodge, 2010), and assessment of different termination criteria during the process (Reddy et al., 2012). Other studies developed effective methods for improving the degree of smoothness of contours, includ- ing the cubic Bezier curve, cubic B-spline, and parabola (Kolingerová et al., 2009; Miao, 2004; Peters et al., 2014; Sun et al., 2000).
  • As the volume of geographic data to be processed in geospatial computation becomes larger and the compu- tational process becomes increasingly complicated, the conventional serial computing paradigm cannot con- tinue to be sufficiently applicable for timely handling of large-scale spatial data in GIS. Thus, it is essential to utilize parallel computing toolsets that can provide powerful computational capability to more efficiently generate contour lines from large-scale DEM data (Hawick et al., 2003; Healey et al., 1998). For instance, Xie (2012) utilized multicore central processing units (CPUs) to accelerate the process and considered opti- mization strategies for increasing the parallel efficiency. In particular, the message passing interface (MPI), which is a widely used standard for message-passing parallel programming, was employed to manage high- performance computing across distributed systems (MPI Forum, 1994).
  • In addition, with the wide application of graphics processing units (GPUs), the process of complicated geospatial computation is being manipulated in an efficient manner with highly concur- rent GPU threads (Guo et al., 2017; Tang, 2013). The compute unified device architecture (CUDA) designed by NVIDIA is implemented for processing on GPUs (Sanders & Kandrot, 2010). It contains a number of multiprocessors and hierarchically organizes the com- puting units as threads, blocks, and grids; it executes parallel computing by coordinating the host and kernel functions (Jacobsen & Senocak, 2013; Lee et al., 2014; Zhou et al., 2017). Tate and Overbye (2008) presented a GPU-based algorithm for power systems to handle scattered data and output gradient contours. Chen et al. (2010) utilized GPUs to reconstruct a contour map from DEM data for digital earth terrain platforms in real time. They also compared the parallel efficiency when these platforms run on different GPU platforms. Tan et al. (2017) introduced a GPU-based technology to solve appearance and performance issues for contour line generation. The inputted raw DEM data can be accelerated with a modern GPU pipeline, and smoothed contour lines can be achieved by using a new contour sorting method.
  • Although current parallel computing techniques have yielded significantly improved performance, two critical issues exist. First, existing parallel strategies usually deal with relatively small-scale DEM data that can be saved in a GPU, and are not suitably applicable for handling large-scale DEM data. Second, the proces- sing of massive DEM data has strong task dependency and thus needs iterative handling to achieve integrated results. Moreover, GPUs are intended to efficiently com- pute massive data using the single-instruction multiple data mode, whereas CPUs are more suitable for hand- ling highly dependent tasks owing to the difference in their hardware architectures. Current strategies merely employ one of them for parallelism, but the advantages of using both techniques in unison to accelerate the process have not yet been exploited.
  • This study employed a typical linear interpolation algo- rithm to generate contours from DEM data, which involves steps for generating interpolated points and then connecting contour lines (Xie, 2012). In this algo- rithm, the value of a raster pixel is used to represent the elevation of its central point.
    When interpolated points are generated, each of the 2 × 2 pixels within the DEM grid is processed in sequence. During this process, the centers of adjacent pixels that share a common edge are connected as line segments; then, target points with a specific elevation value can be linearly interpolated on the boundary of the formed line segments (Figure 1(a)). When contour lines are connected, the interpolated points with the same elevation value in the entire grid are first extracted. They are then connected as contour segments and finally merged together as integrated contour lines (Figure 1(b)). When considering no-data pixels, the middle points of the lines connected by the central points of the no-data pixel and two neighbor pixels are generated and assigned the values of the neighbor pixels. The central point of the grid of 2 × 2 pixels is assigned the average of the other three pixels. The formed middle and central points are connected for further linear interpolation.
  • Then, we employed the Bresenham’s algorithm (Bresenham, 1965), which is commonly used for line drawing in the field of computer graphics, to convert the contour lines into raster at 5 m.
