RouteLAND: An Integrated Method and a Geoprocessing Tool for Characterizing the Dynamic Visual Landscape Along Highways

Links		https://doi.org/10.3390/ijgi14050187

The visual landscape is shaped by the number of people active in an area. Popular locations often see the introduction of more man-made features—such as roads and highways—to support accessibility and functionality. While this can increase popularity by making the area more reachable, it must be managed carefully, as visual experience significantly influences how long people stay and how satisfied they feel. For instance, constructing new accommodations that block a distinctive or valued view may allow more visitors, but could diminish the very reason people were drawn to the area in the first place. This highlights the need for urban planning, urban design, and the built environment sectors to work collaboratively to balance development with the preservation of visual and experiential quality.

The visual landscape can be understood from two main perspectives: the egocentric (human, ground-level experience) and the exocentric (cartographic or 2D map view). While both are important, there is a pressing need to quantitatively capture not just static but also dynamic visual experiences, especially along linear routes like highways.

Some studies have tackled the challenge of accurately estimating the perceived size of landscape elements. For example, Minelli et al. [34] assessed perceived object size (e.g., wind turbines) relative to its area within varying fields of view. Nutsford et al. [35] created a Visual Visibility Index (VVI) that adjusts traditional viewshed analyses by calculating the vertical angular size between an observer's eye level and visible terrain points. By also accounting for horizontal angular size, the VVI effectively captures the visual significance of landscape features from a human (egocentric) perspective.

In recent decades, several studies have focused on visualizing and quantitatively analyzing terrain visibility from a moving observer's perspective along linear routes across various topographies (e.g., ridgelines, valleys) [27,38,39]. These studies used animated maps and explored statistical relationships between topography and viewshed area but did not address the qualitative aspects of landscape composition or account for the actual size of visible/non-visible cells within the horizontal field of view (hFoV).

Another study [41] proposed a comprehensive framework combining multiple indicators (e.g., sky expanse, landmark distribution) to quantify dynamic visual perception along linear urban landscapes (LULs), considering movement modes like walking, cycling, and boating. While this approach captures the continuous and dynamic nature of visual experience, its focus lies in modeling changing cityscape views through photographs or perspective drawings and applying visual evaluation indicators based on scenario preferences.

The case study’s final output was an interactive web map that visualizes dominant landscape elements within the directional field of view (dFoV) for predefined highway segments. The map displays route segments categorized by dominant land use/land cover (LU/LC) on ESRI Gray (dark) and OpenStreetMap (OSM) backgrounds. Users can explore attributes such as LU/LC, DomLand values, and LandChar descriptions, with options to filter segments and measure distances and areas. Zoom levels are restricted (13z–16z) for efficient navigation. The map was developed using open-source geospatial tools, including QGIS (v3.34.12 LTR), QuickMapServices for basemaps, and the qgis2web plugin along with the Leaflet JavaScript library for web deployment.

The RouteLAND tool enables automatic characterization of any route based on the dominant landscape element within the directional field of view (dFoV). By modeling the vistas experienced from moving vehicles, it provides a cartographic representation of the actual landscape composition observed by highway users. Given the high number of observers on highways, this method can support comparative landscape analysis in peri-urban areas and enhance public decision-making for landscape evaluation and sustainable spatial planning. The approach offers a foundation that can be further improved and adapted for various applications and research fields.

Several enhancements can be made to the RouteLAND tool. To increase flexibility and reduce reliance on GIS software and commercial Python packages, future versions could rely solely on open-source Python libraries and be developed in Python 3.x to leverage language improvements. The tool could also be extended to include an automated web map generation feature for better result visualization. Additionally, the current codebase provides a strong foundation for developing either a standalone application or a QGIS plugin with a built-in graphical user interface (GUI).

In landscape research, the RouteLAND tool offers valuable applications for Landscape Character Assessment (LCA). Traditionally focused on the exocentric perspective, LCA could benefit from RouteLAND by shifting toward the egocentric perspective, enabling character assessments along key routes. The method also opens possibilities for evaluating landscape character based on criteria such as naturalness, complexity, and disturbance, as well as for rating visual quality and preferences. Building on earlier work by Dramstad et al. [50], who linked visual preferences to map-based indicators using viewshed analysis, RouteLAND can effectively quantify indicators of landscape composition and structure within the directional field of view (dFoV) along routes.

The RouteLAND method can significantly contribute to Visual Impact Assessment (VIA) and inform alternative design scenarios for improved spatial and landscape planning. While models like GEMMELIM have mapped visual impacts from activities such as surface mining, they lack consideration of the directional field of view (dFoV). RouteLAND can be adapted for dynamic VIA of intrusive projects like quarries, wind farms, or solar farms along routes. It also has potential in route assessment and design, helping identify paths that minimize visual intrusion through cost path analysis based on dFoV visibility. Moreover, RouteLAND can support legislation on VIA by offering a scientifically grounded method for quantifying visual impacts of current or planned developments [51]. On the positive side of VIA, the tool can also be used to maximize exposure to visually appealing or culturally significant landscape features (e.g., lakes, forests, monuments, landmarks). In doing so, it can guide the evaluation and design of routes—by land or sea—that enhance touristic experiences and support sustainable landscape planning.

The RouteLAND method offers a novel opportunity to bridge GISystems and GIScience with the fields of eye movement analysis and visual perception. This interdisciplinary perspective, previously explored by Schirpke et al. [52] and Misthos et al. [24], is strengthened by RouteLAND’s ability to quantify and spatially represent visible landscape elements within the directional field of view (dFoV). Integrating eye-tracking data with geospatial outputs presents a promising avenue for connecting 2D map-based information with the real-time visual experience of observers—especially in dynamic, movement-based scenarios such as traveling along routes.
